Interfaces of Power

Visualizing U.S. Political Use of Google's Advertising, Data, and Content Systems

David Salcido | DSC 209 Data Visualization | UC San Diego

$2.2 Billion
spent on Google political ads since 2018
But who's really spending it? The answer might surprise you.

Every day, you use Google. You search, you watch, you click. But did you know that same platform is also being used to influence your vote, surveil your data, and control what you can see online?

Political actors interact with Google through three "interfaces of power": advertising platforms for persuasion, data requests for surveillance, and content removal requests for censorship. Scroll down to explore patterns you might find surprising.

1. The Persuasion Machine: Political Ad Spending

You might think Democrats and Republicans dominate political advertising. But here's the twist: they're both outspent by a shadow network of PACs, advocacy groups, and ballot measure campaigns. Explore the data below to see who's really paying to reach you.

What surprised us: "Other" advertisers like PACs, advocacy groups, and ballot measure campaigns outspend both major parties combined. That's $1.56 billion from groups you've probably never heard of, versus $359 million from Democrats and $285 million from Republicans. The real players in political advertising aren't the parties at all.

2. The Surveillance Interface: Government Data Requests

While politicians fight over ads, both parties agree on one thing: they want access to your data. The government has made over 1 million requests for user information from Google‚Äîand the number keeps growing, regardless of who's in power.

The pattern: Government requests for user data have grown 17 times over since 2009, from about 3,600 requests to over 60,000 per reporting period. Unlike advertising, this growth doesn't care about party lines. Obama, Trump, Biden: the numbers kept climbing under all of them.

3. The Censorship Mechanism: Content Removal Requests

You might expect government censorship requests to target terrorism or national security threats. But the data tells a different story. Most removal requests are about something far more mundane‚Äîand perhaps more concerning.

The surprise: Defamation accounts for 6,731 requests, more than half of all removals. The government's main reason for asking Google to take down content isn't terrorism or national security. It's protecting someone's reputation. That should make you pause.

4. Your Vote Matters More If You Live Here

Not all votes are created equal. Political campaigns concentrate spending in states where elections are competitive‚Äîmeaning if you live in a "safe" state, you might barely notice a presidential campaign at all.

Look for your state: California leads with $245 million because it has the most people. But the real story is in the swing states. Pennsylvania got $189 million, Michigan $141 million, and Georgia $137 million, all despite having much smaller populations. Where you live determines how hard campaigns work to persuade you.

5. Three Timelines, One Platform

Now let's see all three interfaces together. When aligned on the same timeline, a striking pattern emerges: advertising follows elections, but surveillance never stops.

Notice the contrast: Political advertising spikes before elections, then drops off. You can see it in the gold lines marking 2018, 2020, 2022, and 2024. Surveillance requests, meanwhile, have grown steadily regardless of who's in office. Content removal peaked around 2016 and has actually declined since then. Each interface follows its own rhythm.
