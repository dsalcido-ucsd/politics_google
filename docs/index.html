Interfaces of Power

Visualizing U.S. Political Use of Google's Advertising, Data, and Content Systems

Google serves as a critical infrastructure for political power in America. Political actors interact with Google through three distinct but interconnected systems: advertising platforms for persuasion, data requests for surveillance, and content removal requests for censorship.

This explorable explanation reveals how these "interfaces of power" operate across time, showing patterns that emerge during election cycles, across presidential administrations, and through different political parties.

1. The Persuasion Machine: Political Ad Spending

Political parties spend billions to reach voters through Google's advertising platforms. This chart shows weekly spending by Democratic, Republican, and other advertisers since 2018. Notice the dramatic spikes during election cycles.

Key Insight: "Other" advertisers (PACs, advocacy groups, ballot measures) outspend both major parties combined, representing $1.56B vs. $359M (Democratic) and $285M (Republican). Spending peaks sharply before November elections.

2. The Surveillance Interface: Government Data Requests

The U.S. government regularly requests user information from Google through legal processes like subpoenas and search warrants. This visualization tracks over 1 million requests affecting more than 2 million accounts since 2009.

Key Insight: Government surveillance through Google has grown steadily, from ~3,600 requests in 2009 to over 60,000 per period by 2024. Google's disclosure rate has fluctuated between 70-90% over time.

3. The Censorship Mechanism: Content Removal Requests

Government agencies request Google to remove content for various reasons. This chart categorizes over 12,000 removal requests since 2011, revealing what types of content authorities seek to suppress.

Key Insight: Defamation (6,731 requests) dominates content removal, followed by privacy concerns (1,346) and fraud (1,005). National security requests, while highly publicized, represent a smaller fraction of total removals.

4. Battleground States: Where Political Ad Money Flows

Political advertising concentrates in competitive states. This map reveals which states receive the most political ad spending.

Key Insight: California leads with $245M in spending (large population), followed by Pennsylvania ($189M), Michigan ($141M), and Georgia ($137M) â€” all critical swing states in recent elections.

5. Three Timelines, One Platform

When we align these three interfaces on the same timeline, patterns emerge. Hover over any point to see synchronized data across all three dimensions of power.

Key Insight: Political advertising follows a strong cyclical pattern tied to elections, while surveillance and content removal show steady, long-term growth independent of election cycles.

Understanding the Interfaces

These visualizations reveal how political power operates through Google's infrastructure. Persuasion (advertising) is cyclical, expensive, and partisan. Surveillance (data requests) is persistent, growing, and bipartisan. Censorship (content removal) focuses on individual reputation rather than political dissent.

Together, these three interfaces form a comprehensive picture of how government and political actors leverage one of the world's most powerful information platforms to shape public opinion, gather intelligence, and control online speech.
